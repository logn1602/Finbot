# Debt Management: Taking Control of What You Owe

## Understanding Good Debt vs. Bad Debt

Not all debt is created equal. The distinction matters for prioritization:

- **Productive debt** finances assets that appreciate or generate income: mortgages on property, student loans for high-return degrees, or business loans. These typically carry lower interest rates and tax benefits.
- **Destructive debt** finances consumption that depreciates immediately: credit card balances, payday loans, buy-now-pay-later plans for discretionary items. Interest rates are high, and the purchased items lose value fast.

The goal is not necessarily to be debt-free at all costs — it is to eliminate high-cost debt while using low-cost debt strategically.

## Know Your Numbers

Before you can manage debt, you need a complete picture:

1. List every debt: creditor name, current balance, interest rate (APR), minimum monthly payment, and due date.
2. Calculate your total debt.
3. Calculate your debt-to-income ratio: total monthly debt payments divided by gross monthly income. A ratio above 36 % is a warning sign; above 43 % makes it difficult to qualify for new credit.
4. Identify the highest-interest debt — this is usually the most urgent target.

## The Avalanche Method (Mathematically Optimal)

Pay minimum on all debts, then throw every extra dollar at the debt with the **highest interest rate**. Once that is paid off, roll its payment into the next-highest-rate debt.

**Pros**: Saves the most money in interest over time. Reduces total repayment duration.
**Cons**: If the highest-rate debt has a large balance, progress can feel slow early on.

Example:
- Credit card A: $5,000 at 22 % APR → attack this first
- Credit card B: $2,000 at 18 % APR → second
- Car loan: $12,000 at 6 % APR → last (low rate)

## The Snowball Method (Psychologically Powerful)

Pay minimum on all debts, then throw every extra dollar at the debt with the **smallest balance**, regardless of interest rate. When it is paid off, roll that payment into the next-smallest balance.

**Pros**: Quick wins build momentum and motivation. You eliminate entire accounts faster.
**Cons**: You may pay more in total interest compared to the avalanche method.

**Which should you choose?** Research shows the snowball method has higher completion rates because of its psychological rewards. If you are disciplined and motivated by math, use avalanche. If you need early wins to stay on track, use snowball. Both work — the worst strategy is no strategy.

## Debt Consolidation

Consolidation combines multiple debts into a single loan, ideally at a lower interest rate. Options include:

- **Balance transfer credit cards**: Offer 0 % introductory APR for 12-21 months. Transfer high-interest balances and pay down aggressively during the promo period. Watch for transfer fees (typically 3-5 %).
- **Personal consolidation loans**: Fixed rate, fixed term. Simplifies payments and may lower your blended interest rate.
- **Home equity loans/lines of credit**: Lower rates because they are secured by your home. Risk: you can lose your home if you default.

Consolidation only works if you stop adding new debt. Otherwise, you end up with the consolidation loan plus new balances — worse than where you started.

## Negotiating with Creditors

If you are struggling to make payments:

1. **Call before you miss a payment**, not after. Creditors are more willing to help proactive borrowers.
2. **Ask for a lower interest rate**. If you have a history of on-time payments, many issuers will reduce your APR by 2-5 points.
3. **Request a hardship plan**. Most major lenders offer temporary reduced payments, lower rates, or fee waivers during financial hardship.
4. **Negotiate settlements** on charged-off accounts. Creditors may accept 40-60 % of the balance as payment in full. Get any agreement in writing before paying.

## Avoiding the Debt Trap

- **Build an emergency fund** before aggressively paying debt. Without cash reserves, the next emergency goes right back on the credit card.
- **Stop using credit cards for daily spending** while in payoff mode. Switch to debit or cash to break the cycle.
- **Unsubscribe from marketing emails** and unfollow shopping accounts on social media. Reduce temptation at the source.
- **Use a 48-hour rule** for non-essential purchases over a set amount. If you still want it after two days, buy it with cash.

## Student Loan Strategies

Student loans have unique features:

- **Income-driven repayment plans** cap payments at a percentage of discretionary income.
- **Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF)** forgives remaining balances after 120 qualifying payments for eligible public sector workers.
- **Refinancing** can lower interest rates but eliminates federal protections like income-driven plans and forgiveness eligibility. Only refinance federal loans if you are certain you will not need those protections.
- **Pay interest during grace periods** to prevent capitalization (interest being added to principal).

## Credit Score Impact

Debt management directly affects your credit score:

- **Payment history** (35 % of score): Never miss a payment. Set up autopay for at least the minimum.
- **Credit utilization** (30 % of score): Keep credit card balances below 30 % of your credit limit — below 10 % is ideal.
- **Length of credit history** (15 %): Do not close your oldest accounts after paying them off.
- **Closing paid-off cards**: Reduces available credit, which increases utilization ratio. Keep cards open with zero balance unless they have annual fees.

## Key Takeaway

Debt repayment is a marathon, not a sprint. Pick a strategy (avalanche or snowball), automate your payments, and track progress monthly. Every dollar of high-interest debt you eliminate is a guaranteed return equal to that debt's interest rate — often better than any investment you could make.
