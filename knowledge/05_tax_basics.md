# Tax Basics: Keeping More of What You Earn

## Why Taxes Matter for Personal Finance

Taxes are often your single largest expense — larger than housing for many earners. Understanding the basics allows you to legally minimize your tax burden and keep more money working toward your goals. You do not need to be a tax expert, but knowing the key concepts can save you thousands of dollars every year.

## How Income Tax Works

Most countries use a **progressive tax system**, meaning your income is taxed in brackets. Only the income within each bracket is taxed at that bracket's rate — your entire income is not taxed at the highest rate you reach.

Example (simplified):
- First $10,000 → taxed at 10 % = $1,000
- Next $30,000 → taxed at 22 % = $6,600
- Next $45,000 → taxed at 24 % = $10,800
- Total tax on $85,000 income = $18,400 (effective rate ≈ 21.6 %)

Your **marginal tax rate** is the rate on your last dollar of income (24 % in this example). Your **effective tax rate** is total tax divided by total income (21.6 %). The effective rate is always lower than the marginal rate.

## Deductions and Credits

**Tax deductions** reduce your taxable income. If you are in the 22 % bracket and claim a $1,000 deduction, you save $220 in taxes.

**Tax credits** reduce your tax bill directly, dollar for dollar. A $1,000 credit saves exactly $1,000 regardless of your bracket. Credits are more valuable than deductions of the same amount.

### Standard Deduction vs. Itemizing
- The **standard deduction** is a flat amount everyone can claim (adjusted annually for inflation).
- **Itemizing** means listing specific deductions (mortgage interest, state taxes, charitable donations, medical expenses above a threshold).
- Choose whichever gives you the larger deduction. Most taxpayers benefit from the standard deduction.

## Tax-Advantaged Accounts

The most powerful legal tax reduction tool is contributing to tax-advantaged accounts:

- **Pre-tax retirement accounts** (Traditional 401k, Traditional IRA): Contributions reduce this year's taxable income. You pay tax when you withdraw in retirement, ideally at a lower rate.
- **Roth accounts** (Roth IRA, Roth 401k): No deduction now, but all growth and withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free.
- **HSA (Health Savings Account)**: Contributions are deductible, growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for medical expenses are tax-free — a triple benefit.
- **529 Education Plans**: Contributions grow tax-free; withdrawals for qualified education expenses are tax-free.

**Strategy**: If you expect your tax rate to be higher in retirement (e.g., you are early in your career), Roth contributions are advantageous. If your rate is high now and likely to be lower later, pre-tax contributions save more.

## Capital Gains Tax

When you sell an investment for more than you paid, the profit is a capital gain:

- **Short-term capital gains** (assets held less than one year): Taxed at your ordinary income rate.
- **Long-term capital gains** (assets held more than one year): Taxed at preferential rates, typically 0 %, 15 %, or 20 % depending on income.

**Implication**: Holding investments for at least one year before selling can significantly reduce your tax bill. This is another reason long-term investing beats frequent trading.

## Tax-Loss Harvesting

If an investment has declined in value, you can sell it to realize a capital loss. That loss can offset capital gains dollar for dollar, reducing your tax liability. If losses exceed gains, you can deduct up to $3,000 of net losses against ordinary income per year, carrying forward any excess to future years.

**Important**: The wash-sale rule prevents you from buying the same or substantially identical investment within 30 days before or after the sale. You can buy a similar (but not identical) fund to maintain market exposure.

## Self-Employment Tax Considerations

Self-employed individuals pay both the employer and employee portions of Social Security and Medicare taxes (approximately 15.3 % on net self-employment income). Strategies to manage this:

- **Deduct business expenses** to reduce net self-employment income.
- **Contribute to a Solo 401k or SEP IRA** — higher contribution limits than individual plans.
- **Consider an S-Corporation election** if your income justifies it — can reduce self-employment tax by splitting income between salary and distributions.
- **Make quarterly estimated tax payments** to avoid underpayment penalties.

## Common Tax Mistakes

1. **Not withholding enough**: A big refund feels good but means you gave the government an interest-free loan. Aim for a refund near zero.
2. **Missing deductions**: Home office, student loan interest, educator expenses, moving expenses for military — many deductions are commonly overlooked.
3. **Ignoring state and local taxes**: Some states have no income tax; others have rates above 10 %. This affects where you live and work.
4. **Not keeping records**: Save receipts for deductible expenses. Digital scans organized by year and category are sufficient.
5. **Filing late without an extension**: Penalties and interest accrue. If you cannot file on time, always file an extension — it is free and gives you six more months.

## Tax Planning Through the Year

Do not wait until tax season to think about taxes:

- **January**: Review previous year's withholding. Adjust W-4 if you consistently get large refunds or owe large amounts.
- **Quarterly**: If self-employed, make estimated payments by the deadlines (April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15).
- **October-December**: Harvest tax losses, make charitable donations, maximize retirement contributions before year-end.
- **Year-round**: Track deductible expenses as they occur. A spreadsheet or expense tracker is sufficient.

## Key Takeaway

Tax planning is not about cheating the system — it is about using the rules the government created to encourage saving, investing, and responsible financial behavior. Every dollar you legally save on taxes is a dollar that can go toward your financial goals.
